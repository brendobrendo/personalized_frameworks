# The Origin of Amazon Web Services (AWS)

Amazon Web Services (AWS) began as an internal project to solve Amazon's own technology bottlenecks. By modularizing their sprawling e-commerce infrastructure into APIs, leadership realized they could sell these standardized computing and storage building blocks to external developers, ultimately revolutionizing the global tech industry.

## The Internal Bottleneck

In the early 2000s, Amazon was growing exponentially as an e-commerce platform. It was also quietly running e-commerce for *other* companies. Through a program called Merchant.com, Amazon supplied the website technology—and often the fulfillment and customer service—behind the online stores of major retailers like Target and Toys "R" Us, who kept their own branding out front. That work was revealing. Building bespoke storefronts for big outside clients forced Amazon to confront just how much effort went into the plumbing—and how every project seemed to rebuild the same foundations from scratch.

The same pain showed up internally: Amazon's engineering teams were constantly bogged down by the unglamorous, time-consuming task of building foundational IT infrastructure—servers, storage, databases—for every new project. Engineers were spending too much time on setup rather than customer-facing innovation.

## The Service-Oriented Pivot

To survive this scaling challenge, Amazon required a massive internal overhaul. They transitioned their monolithic codebase into a decentralized, "service-oriented architecture." This meant every internal team had to expose their data and systems exclusively through web interfaces (APIs). The Merchant.com experience had already pointed the way: the more Amazon treated its capabilities as standardized, reusable services rather than one-off custom builds, the faster everyone could move.

## The "Internet OS" Vision

During a review of their IT systems, leaders realized that every external startup and developer was facing the exact same infrastructure hurdles Amazon had—and that Merchant.com had already proven outside companies were hungry for Amazon's technology. In 2003, Amazon executives Jeff Bezos, Andy Jassy, and Rick Dalzell mapped out a vision to build an "Internet OS." They decided to commercialize these standardized infrastructure primitives so that anyone, from a college student to an enterprise, could build on Amazon's technology.

## Official Launch

After releasing early, basic developer tools (like APIs for the Amazon product catalog in 2002), AWS officially launched its modern cloud infrastructure in 2006. The first piece, the Simple Queue Service (SQS), arrived in 2004—but the launch that changed everything came in 2006 with the Simple Storage Service (S3) and Elastic Compute Cloud (EC2). This allowed companies to rent virtual computers and storage on demand, effectively transforming tech from an upfront capital expense into a variable utility.

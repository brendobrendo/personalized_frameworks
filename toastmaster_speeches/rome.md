# Toastmasters Speech Outline  
## Rome: A City Built in Layers

### Opening (10–15 seconds)

> “Last week I walked through Rome and realized something strange — the city feels like time is stacked on top of itself. You turn a corner and suddenly you’re standing in 2,000 years of history at once.”

Tonight I want to tell the story of Rome in **four layers**:

1. Classical Rome  
2. Medieval Rome  
3. Renaissance Rome  
4. Modern Rome

I’ll use the **buildings I saw during my trip** to tell the story of each period.

---

# Part 1 — Classical Rome  
## Republic → Empire

**Key insight:**  
Rome was **not always an empire**. For its first **500 years**, it was a **Republic**.

### Roman Republic (509–27 BC)

- Governed by **senators and elected magistrates**
- Expanded throughout the **Mediterranean world**
- Built practical infrastructure to support a growing civilization

### Architecture Examples

- **Temple of Portunus** → example of Republican-era temple architecture  
- **Roman cobblestone roads** → engineering that allowed Rome to control vast territory  

### Transition to Empire

Political instability and civil wars eventually ended the Republic.

- **Augustus becomes the first emperor**
- Rome becomes an **Imperial superpower**

### Architecture of Imperial Rome

- **Pantheon** → engineering masterpiece and statement of imperial power  
- **Colosseum** → public spectacle designed to impress the empire

**Key idea:**

> The Republic built **temples and roads**.  
> The Empire built **monuments meant to impress the world**.

---

# Part 2 — Medieval Rome  
## The Church Fills the Power Vacuum

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, Rome changed dramatically.

### Population Collapse

- Ancient Rome population: **over 1 million**
- Early medieval population: **20,000–40,000**

Much of the ancient city fell into ruin.

### San Giorgio in Velabro

This church reveals something fascinating about the time period.

Medieval builders used a technique called **spolia**:

- Stones and columns were **reused from ancient Roman buildings**
- New churches were literally built **from the remains of the old empire**

**Interpretation:**

> Medieval Rome was not building entirely new structures.  
> It was building **Christian Rome from the ruins of pagan Rome**.

### Shift in Power

With the collapse of imperial government, the **Catholic Church became the central institution of authority** in the city.

---

# Part 3 — Renaissance Rome  
## The Rise of Vatican Power

During the Renaissance, the papacy transformed Rome again.

Popes began investing heavily in:

- Architecture  
- Art  
- Urban renewal

Their goal was to make Rome the **spiritual capital of the Christian world**.

### Major Projects

- **St. Peter’s Basilica**
- **Vatican City**
- Major works by **Michelangelo** and **Raphael**

**Key idea:**

> Renaissance popes looked at the ruins of ancient Rome and decided to rebuild the city to rival its former glory.

In many ways:

> Renaissance Rome became the **Church’s version of an empire**.

---

# Part 4 — Rome and the Italian Nation State  
## Modern Rome

Rome’s final transformation happened in the **19th century**.

### Italian Unification

- **1870 — Rome becomes part of the Kingdom of Italy**
- The Papal States come to an end
- Rome becomes the **capital of a modern nation**

### Major Construction

To modernize the city, Italy built:

- **Vittorio Emanuele II Monument (Altare della Patria)**
- New government buildings
- Wide boulevards and redesigned urban spaces

Rome now had to function not just as a religious center, but as the **capital of Italy**.

---

# Conclusion (15–20 seconds)

What surprised me most about Rome is that it **isn’t just one city**.

It is actually **four cities layered on top of each other**:

- A **Republican city**
- An **Imperial city**
- A **Medieval Christian city**
- A **Modern national capital**

> When you walk through Rome today, you’re really walking through **all of them at once**.

# Monthly Reports

## Homes

### Existing Home Sales
The National Association of Realtors’ (NAR) monthly Existing-Home Sales report is one of the most widely watched indicators of the U.S. housing market. It tracks completed sales of previously owned homes (single-family homes, condos, and co-ops), along with median prices, inventory levels, months of supply, days on market, first-time buyer participation, cash purchases, and other market metrics. The data is collected through a monthly survey of a representative sample of local Realtor boards and Multiple Listing Services (MLSs) across the country, which NAR then seasonally adjusts and annualizes to estimate national activity. The report does break the data down by the four major U.S. Census regions—Northeast, Midwest, South, and West—and provides separate sales and price statistics for each region. It is especially useful to economists, homebuilders, real estate agents, mortgage lenders, investors, policymakers, and financial markets because it provides an early read on housing demand, affordability, inventory conditions, and broader economic momentum.

### Housing Starts
The monthly Housing Starts report, formally called the Census Bureau and HUD's **New Residential Construction Report**, measures the supply side of the housing market by tracking new privately owned housing units that are authorized, started, under construction, and completed. The report includes three major sections: **building permits** (future construction intentions), **housing starts** (construction has begun, defined as excavation for the foundation), and **housing completions** (homes ready for occupancy). Data is collected through the Census Bureau's Building Permits Survey and the jointly administered Census-HUD Survey of Construction, which samples local permitting offices and construction projects nationwide. The report provides national statistics and breaks results down by the four Census regions—**Northeast, Midwest, South, and West**—and also separates single-family from multifamily construction. Economists, homebuilders, construction suppliers, mortgage lenders, real estate investors, banks, and Federal Reserve analysts follow it closely because housing starts are considered a leading economic indicator: builders generally begin projects only when they expect future housing demand to justify the investment.

### Pending Home Sales
The National Association of Realtors' (NAR) monthly Pending Home Sales report measures housing demand by tracking signed contracts to purchase existing homes that have not yet closed. Its headline metric, the **Pending Home Sales Index (PHSI)**, is based on contract activity and is designed to be a leading indicator of future existing-home sales, since most transactions close one to two months after a contract is signed. The data is collected from a large sample of Multiple Listing Services (MLSs) and Realtor associations across the country and is seasonally adjusted to create the index. The report includes national results as well as regional breakdowns for the **Northeast, Midwest, South, and West**, showing month-over-month and year-over-year changes in contract signings. It is most useful to economists, real estate professionals, mortgage lenders, homebuilders, investors, and policymakers because it provides one of the earliest signals of changes in housing demand before they appear in the Existing Home Sales report. In a typical housing-market sequence, pending sales tend to move first, existing-home sales follow a month or two later, and related economic activity follows afterward. National Association of Realtors publishes the report monthly.

### New Home Sales
The monthly New Home Sales report, formally called **New Residential Sales**, is produced jointly by the United States Census Bureau and United States Department of Housing and Urban Development and measures the demand for newly built single-family homes. Unlike Existing Home Sales, a sale is counted when a purchase contract is signed or a deposit is accepted—not when the transaction closes—making it a relatively timely indicator of housing demand. The report includes annualized sales rates, median and average sales prices, inventory levels (homes available for sale), and months of supply. Data is collected through the Survey of Construction, which samples homebuilders and building permit records nationwide. The report breaks results down by the four Census regions—**Northeast, Midwest, South, and West**—and often provides information on homes sold by stage of construction (not started, under construction, completed). It is most useful to economists, homebuilders, construction suppliers, mortgage lenders, real estate investors, and policymakers because it offers an early look at demand for new housing, builder confidence, inventory conditions, and future residential construction activity. A useful way to think about it is that **Pending Home Sales tracks contracts for existing homes, Existing Home Sales tracks completed sales of existing homes, and New Home Sales tracks contracts for newly built homes.**

### FHFA Home Price Index
The **FHFA Home Price Index (HPI)** is a monthly report produced by the Federal Housing Finance Agency that measures changes in U.S. single-family home prices over time. Unlike reports that track sales volume, the HPI focuses on **home price appreciation and depreciation** using a "repeat-sales" methodology, which compares the sale prices of the same properties across multiple transactions to isolate changes in market value. The data comes from millions of mortgage transactions purchased or guaranteed by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. The report includes national, regional, state, and metropolitan-area indexes, making it one of the most geographically detailed housing reports available. It is most useful to economists, investors, mortgage lenders, appraisers, policymakers, and homeowners because it helps answer the question: **"How fast are home prices rising or falling?"** A useful way to think about it is that Existing Home Sales, New Home Sales, and Pending Home Sales measure **housing activity**, while the FHFA HPI measures **housing prices**.

### S&P CS Home Prices
The monthly **S&P CoreLogic Case-Shiller Home Price Index** is one of the most closely watched measures of U.S. home-price trends. Published by S&P Dow Jones Indices and based on data from CoreLogic, it measures changes in the prices of existing single-family homes using a repeat-sales methodology that compares the sale prices of the same properties over time. The report's headline measures are the **10-City Composite**, **20-City Composite**, and **National Home Price Index**, along with individual indexes for major metropolitan areas. Unlike the FHFA HPI, which is based primarily on mortgages acquired by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the Case-Shiller index includes a broader range of transactions and is particularly focused on large urban housing markets. The report is released monthly using a three-month moving average to reduce volatility, which means it tends to lag market turning points somewhat. It is most useful to economists, investors, policymakers, real estate professionals, and financial markets because it provides a widely respected benchmark for home-price appreciation and regional housing-market strength. A useful distinction is that **FHFA HPI provides broader geographic coverage (including states and many metro areas), while Case-Shiller is often viewed as the premier measure of home-price trends in major U.S. cities.**

## Jobs

### Initial Jobless Claims

The **Initial Jobless Claims** report, published weekly by the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL), measures the number of people filing for unemployment insurance benefits for the first time. Because workers typically file for benefits soon after losing their jobs, it is one of the timeliest indicators of labor market conditions and often provides the earliest signal that employment is strengthening or weakening before monthly employment data is released. The report also includes **Continuing Claims**, which measure the number of people already receiving unemployment benefits, providing insight into how easily unemployed workers are finding new jobs. The data is collected directly from state unemployment insurance agencies, which report the number of new and continuing claims each week. The report provides **national totals**, **state-by-state data**, and information for **Puerto Rico and the U.S. Virgin Islands**, although the headline figure most closely followed by financial markets is the seasonally adjusted national total. It is released **every Thursday at 8:30 a.m. Eastern Time**, making it one of the highest-frequency economic indicators available. Economists, investors, Federal Reserve officials, policymakers, and financial markets monitor it closely because sudden increases in claims often provide one of the earliest warnings of labor market deterioration and, by extension, broader economic weakness.

### Employment Report

The monthly **Employment Situation Report**, commonly referred to as the **Jobs Report**, is produced jointly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) and the Department of Labor (DOL) and is widely considered the single most important monthly indicator of the health of the U.S. labor market. It combines the results of **two separate surveys**. The **Current Employment Statistics (CES) survey**, also called the **Establishment Survey**, collects payroll data from approximately **121,000 businesses and government agencies covering about 629,000 individual worksites** to estimate **nonfarm payroll employment, average hourly earnings, average weekly hours worked, and employment by industry**. The **Current Population Survey (CPS)**, also known as the **Household Survey**, interviews approximately **60,000 households** each month to estimate the **unemployment rate, labor force participation rate, employment-to-population ratio, multiple jobholders, self-employment, and demographic employment statistics**. Together, the two surveys provide a comprehensive picture of hiring, wages, hours worked, and labor force dynamics.

The report is released **monthly, typically on the first Friday of the month at 8:30 a.m. Eastern Time**, and provides data at multiple geographic levels. The headline payroll and unemployment figures are **national**, while detailed employment estimates are also available by **industry**, **occupation**, **age**, **sex**, **race**, and **educational attainment**. Separate BLS programs extend the geographic detail to **states**, **metropolitan areas**, and **counties** through related employment releases. Because it captures both job creation and labor force participation, the Employment Report is followed closely by the Federal Reserve, financial markets, economists, businesses, policymakers, and investors. Payroll growth helps answer **"How many jobs are being created?"**, while the Household Survey answers **"How many people are working or looking for work?"** Together they provide the most complete monthly snapshot of employment conditions in the United States.

# Big Bank Q2 2026 Earnings — July 14, 2026

Five of the largest U.S. banks reported second-quarter results before the market open today, in what amounted to a single-day stress test for sentiment on the financial sector — and nearly every one of them blew past expectations, driven by a historic surge in trading and investment banking revenue.

---

## JPMorgan Chase

- **EPS:** $7.70 (adjusted: $6.14) vs. ~$5.50–$5.59 consensus — the bank's **highest quarterly profit ever**
- **Revenue:** $58.0 billion managed revenue (up 27% YoY), boosted by a $4.6 billion gain on its Visa stake
- **Every line of business posted record revenue**; Equity Markets revenue jumped 86% YoY to $6.0 billion, Fixed Income rose 6%, total Markets revenue hit $12.1 billion — a new quarterly trading record
- Investment banking fees climbed 30% to their highest level since 2021
- CEO Jamie Dimon said AI has helped JPMorgan **cut staffing by up to 40% in certain roles**, though most affected employees were redeployed elsewhere in the firm — one of the more direct acknowledgments yet from a major bank CEO of AI's effect on headcount
- Fixed income showed some softness in commodities trading, one of the few blemishes in an otherwise record quarter

## Bank of America

- **EPS:** $1.21 vs. $1.13 estimate; **Revenue:** $31.56 billion, up 14.2% YoY (beat by 2.6%)
- **Net income:** $9.1 billion; Return on tangible common equity of 17.0%
- CEO Brian Moynihan called it one of the strongest quarters to date, noting **"every business segment reported double-digit net income growth and strong returns on equity"**
- Moynihan tied the strength directly to the macro backdrop: **"the U.S. economy has proved more durable than expected, supported by the strong consumer, ongoing AI-driven investments across the board, and easing energy costs, though inflation and tighter monetary policy remain key risks."**
- BofA's own internal card-spending data (released alongside earnings) showed aggregate household card spending up 6.3% YoY in June — the strongest reading since April 2022 — though the bank flagged that some of this was inflated by World Cup-related spending and one-off promotions

## Wells Fargo

- **EPS:** $2.00 (GAAP) / $1.96, vs. ~$1.73 estimate (beat by $0.23–$0.28); **Net income:** $6.4 billion
- **Revenue:** $22.6 billion, up from $20.8 billion a year earlier, beating estimates by roughly $770 million
- Return on average tangible common equity rose to 16.1% in the first half of 2026, up from 14.4% a year earlier, with revenue-related expenses rising modestly and headcount declining
- CFO Mike Santomassimo characterized **consumer trends as "very resilient" despite oil-price volatility**, but flagged a risk: *"If inflation ends up being much higher than people expect, or rates end up having to go much higher than people expect... that will have some kind of impact on consumers and the overall economy. But we just haven't seen that start to materialize yet."*
- Wells Fargo shares slipped slightly after the report despite the beat, as investors focused on margin and loan-growth trends rather than the headline number

## Goldman Sachs

- **EPS:** $20.98, more than 40% above the ~$14.10–$14.51 consensus; **Net revenues:** $20.34 billion; **Net earnings:** $6.63 billion (vs. $3.72 billion a year ago)
- **Record quarter** across nearly every business line: Global Banking & Markets net revenues hit a record $15.52 billion; Equities revenue surged 72% YoY to $7.42 billion (a third straight record-breaking quarter, per Bloomberg); FICC revenue rose 32% to $4.59 billion
- **Investment banking fees jumped 55% YoY to $3.40 billion**, driven by a 130% surge in equity underwriting and a 75% rise in debt underwriting
- Asset & Wealth Management revenue rose to $4.60 billion on record fees and record AUS of $4.04 trillion
- CEO David Solomon said the bank's **deals backlog is at its highest level in five years**, and described AI as **"transformational" but as a technology that enhances talent rather than replacing people** — a notably different framing than JPMorgan's Dimon
- Goldman raised its quarterly dividend to $5.00/share (from $4.50) and returned $5.36 billion to shareholders in the quarter; stock rose more than 7% in early trading, among the strongest reactions of the five banks

## Citigroup

- **EPS:** $3.15, topping **all 20 analyst estimates** compiled by Bloomberg (consensus ~$2.62–$2.74); **Net income:** $5.8 billion (up 45% YoY)
- **Revenue:** $24.77–$24.8 billion, up 14% YoY — Citi's **best quarterly revenue in a decade**
- Equities trading revenue surged 45% YoY to $2.3 billion (prime balances up nearly 60%), though this trailed the growth posted by JPMorgan and Goldman; fixed income revenue rose 7% to $4.7 billion
- Four of five main business segments beat estimates; CFO Gonzalo Luchetti acknowledged Citi "had not moved quickly enough" to build out its equities franchise relative to peers
- Return on tangible common equity hit 13%, at the top end of Citi's 2027–2028 target range — a sign CEO Jane Fraser's multi-year restructuring is gaining traction
- Fraser described Citi as **"a source of financial strength and trust for our clients during uncertain times"**

## The AI Boom's Fingerprints on Bank Earnings

AI showed up in this earnings season in two distinct ways — as a **revenue tailwind** and as a **labor disruptor**:

- **Financing the buildout:** Record investment banking fees across all five banks (up 30–55% YoY) were driven substantially by a wave of debt and equity underwriting tied to the AI infrastructure boom, as hyperscalers, data-center operators, and AI-adjacent companies raise unprecedented sums of capital. With hyperscaler AI capex running at roughly **$700 billion** for 2026 alone, banks' capital-markets and advisory arms are direct beneficiaries of that financing activity.
- **A double-edged narrative on jobs:** JPMorgan's Dimon explicitly credited AI with helping the bank cut staffing by up to 40% in certain roles — a striking admission from the sector's most influential CEO. Goldman's Solomon pushed a softer framing, describing AI as additive to talent rather than a replacement. This split (automation-driven headcount reduction vs. augmentation) mirrors the broader debate playing out across corporate America and is itself a variable banks will be watching in future consumer and commercial lending trends, since AI-driven job displacement in white-collar roles could eventually show up in credit performance.
- **Macro backdrop:** Dimon has separately pointed to the roughly $700 billion in AI-related spending from major tech companies as one of the key forces sustaining the current bull market, alongside low unemployment (~4.3%) and ~2% GDP growth — while also warning that elevated asset prices tied partly to AI enthusiasm raise the risk of a sharper correction down the road.

## Tariffs and Global Trade

- Tariff-driven uncertainty has been a running theme all year (the April 2026 "reciprocal tariff" shock, plus renewed pressure from the Strait of Hormuz conflict), and this quarter's results suggest banks have so far absorbed that volatility without major credit deterioration.
- Dimon has repeatedly flagged **"trade uncertainty"** as one of several "increasingly complex" risks facing the economy, alongside geopolitical tensions, energy price volatility, and large global fiscal deficits — a framing he's maintained across recent quarters rather than escalating.
- Citigroup's institutional franchise — including its Treasury and Trade Solutions business, which finances global trade flows — remains a key swing factor for the bank given its unusually large cross-border footprint (94 countries, 90% of the Fortune 500 as clients); continued growth there this quarter is a signal that global trade financing has held up despite tariff volatility, though the broader trade data (see the May international trade report, where the deficit widened sharply) shows real disruption in underlying trade flows even if bank financing volumes have stayed resilient.
- Wells Fargo's Santomassimo tied tariff/oil-driven inflation risk directly to the interest-rate outlook, essentially arguing that trade-related price pressure — not weak demand — is the more likely channel through which tariffs could eventually hit the consumer.

## Affluent vs. Mass-Market Consumer: The "K-Shaped" Divide

Bank of America's internal card-spending data, published alongside its earnings, offered one of the clearest looks yet at diverging consumer fortunes:

- Aggregate card spending rose 6.3% YoY in June — the strongest pace since April 2022 — but the report noted a **partial narrowing** of the long-running "K-shape": in June, **lower-income households' after-tax wage growth actually rose above that of middle-income households** for the first time in the series, a notable shift after roughly a year in which higher-income wage and spending growth had consistently outpaced everyone else.
- That said, the broader-year pattern documented by BofA Institute has been one of a widening gap: higher-income households have posted markedly stronger card-spending and wage growth than middle- and lower-income cohorts through most of 2026, a divergence reinforced by the OBBBA tax changes (whose benefits skew toward the top of the income distribution) and by cuts to SNAP/Medicaid eligibility that weigh on lower-income households.
- Evidence of the split shows up in consumer behavior: airlines have leaned into premium seating for affluent travelers who keep spending regardless of macro conditions, while chains like McDonald's have rolled out more value/discount options to win back budget-conscious consumers — a pattern consistent with what banks are seeing in their own card data (rising credit card utilization and a growing share of minimum-payment-only accounts among lower-income cardholders, even as overall credit quality remains historically solid).
- None of the big five banks flagged a broad-based consumer credit deterioration this quarter — delinquencies remain historically low and provisions for credit losses stayed contained — but the underlying wage and spending data suggests the resilience of the "average" consumer masks a real bifurcation between affluent households (who are spending freely, aided by wealth gains and favorable tax treatment) and lower/middle-income households (who are more exposed to inflation, tighter government support, and job-market churn).

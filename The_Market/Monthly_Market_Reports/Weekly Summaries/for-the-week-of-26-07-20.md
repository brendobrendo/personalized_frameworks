# June 2026 Import/Export Price Index — Summary

**Release date:** Friday, July 17, 2026
**Source:** U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)

## Headline Numbers

| Metric | June 2026 | Prior Month | 12-Month Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Import prices | +0.3% | +1.7% (May) | +7.1% (largest since Aug 2022) |
| Export prices | -0.6% | +1.2% (May) | +10.2% |

Export prices posted their first monthly decline since May 2025.

## What Drove Imports

- **Fuel/lubricants:** -0.4% in June (first drop since January), after a 12.6% surge in May
  - Petroleum: -0.7%
  - Natural gas: +9.2%
- **Nonfuel imports:** +0.4% in June, following +0.7% in May, led by nonfuel industrial supplies and materials, capital goods, and consumer goods excluding automotives
- **12-month fuel/lubricant prices:** +44.1% (petroleum +45.4%, natural gas +92.9%)
- **By country:**
  - China: +0.9% in June — largest monthly advance since January 2008; +1.3% over 12 months, largest over-the-year gain since November 2022
  - Japan: -0.6% in June, following +0.3% (May) and +0.5% (April)

## What Drove Exports

- **Nonagricultural exports:** -0.7% in June, after +1.2% (May) and +3.7% (April)
  - Nonagricultural industrial supplies and materials: -2.1% in June, after +2.4% in May — lower petroleum and nonferrous metals prices outweighed higher natural gas prices
  - Partial offset from higher capital goods, consumer goods ex-automotives, and automotive vehicle prices
- **Agricultural exports:** +0.2% in June, following +0.9% in May; no monthly decline since December 2025; +4.6% over 12 months on higher soybean, meat, and other food prices
- **Finished goods:** consumer goods ex-automotives +0.3%; automotive vehicles, parts, and engines +0.1%

## Bottom Line

Energy dominated the import side — petroleum eased while natural gas spiked. The export decline was concentrated in industrial supplies and materials rather than a broad pullback. Both indexes remain elevated year-over-year (imports +7.1%, exports +10.2%), consistent with ongoing tariff and geopolitical effects on trade pricing.

**Next release:** July 2026 data, due August 18, 2026.

# Connection to the March 2026 International Trade Report

**Note on timing:** The trade report released May 5, 2026 covered **March 2026** data — a three-month lag from the June price index above. These are separate releases (BLS price index vs. BEA/Census trade balance) tracking different things, but they connect in two ways.

## 1. Methodological Link

BLS's import/export price indexes are the deflators BEA uses to convert nominal trade dollars into "real" (chain-weighted) trade statistics. In other words, the price index doesn't just describe a parallel trend — it's a direct input into how the trade balance's real, inflation-adjusted figures are constructed.

## 2. What the March Trade Report Showed

- **Goods and services deficit:** $60.3B in March, up $2.5B from $57.8B in February (revised)
- **Exports:** $320.9B, up $6.2B from February
- **Imports:** $381.2B, up $8.7B from February
- **Goods deficit:** +$4.1B to $88.7B
- **Services surplus:** +$1.6B to $28.4B

On the export side, energy commodities were already active drivers in March: industrial supplies and materials exports rose $5.0B, with crude oil up $2.8B and other petroleum products up $1.7B.

## 3. The Through-Line: Energy

Petroleum/energy pricing is the common thread across both reports, even with the time gap:

- **March (trade values):** Crude oil and petroleum products were pushing export values higher.
- **June (trade prices):** Petroleum import prices eased (-0.7%), but natural gas prices surged (+9.2% monthly, +92.9% over 12 months) — becoming the dominant energy story by June.

So the same commodity category (energy) is driving both datasets, but the specific driver shifted from petroleum in March toward natural gas by June. Because of the reporting lag, the June price data doesn't directly deflate the March trade figures — the tighter apples-to-apples comparison would be the **March 2026 Import/Export Price Index** (released in April), which is what actually feeds into the March trade report's real dollar values.

# Economic Report Summaries — July 2026

## June ISM Non-Manufacturing (Services) PMI

The Services PMI registered **54.0%** in June, down 0.5 points from May's 54.5%, marking the **24th consecutive month of expansion** for the services sector (any reading above 50 signals growth). Business Activity slipped to 55.4% from 57.7%, New Orders eased to 55.1% from 57.3%, but Employment jumped 3.3 points to 51.2% — a notable move back into expansion after three straight months of contraction. Prices Paid fell to 67.7%, its lowest reading in four months, though still elevated by historical standards, with respondents citing tariff- and Hormuz-conflict-related fuel, diesel, and freight costs. Nine commodities were reported "in short supply" in June, up from five in May — all of them tied to data center construction.

**Short/long-term trends:** Services activity crashed to record lows in the initial 2020 COVID shock, then rebounded sharply through 2021–2022 on pent-up demand and stimulus, before settling into a choppier pattern over the past two years as the sector wrestled with sticky inflation, high rates, and — this year — tariff and geopolitical shocks (the Iran/Strait of Hormuz situation). The current 54.0 reading fits a "resilient but cooling" pattern: growth continues, but momentum has moderated from spring highs, and the persistent employment contraction earlier this year (only now reversing) has been one of the more unusual features of this cycle — output holding up while hiring lagged.

**AI tie-in:** The report explicitly flags data-center-related material shortages, and the Utilities and Information industries have logged more than six months of continuous expansion, largely attributed to AI-driven demand. With hyperscalers directing an estimated **$700+ billion** into AI infrastructure in 2026, the ripple effects are showing up directly in ISM services data — through elevated backlogs, supply constraints on data-center inputs, and stronger activity in the Professional/Scientific/Technical Services category, where firms cited AI-adjacent infrastructure, resilience, and environmental project demand as a source of strength.

## May International Trade Report (Census/BEA, FT-900)

The U.S. goods and services trade deficit **widened sharply to $77.6 billion in May**, up $23.0 billion from a revised $54.6 billion in April. Exports fell to $317.7 billion (down $10.5 billion), while imports rose to $395.3 billion (up $12.5 billion). The goods deficit widened to $106.5 billion; the services surplus edged up to $28.9 billion. Notably, exports of capital goods, computers, and computer accessories all declined month-over-month — a possible early signal of softening in tech-related trade flows — while crude oil exports rose. Year-to-date, the deficit is still down 40.6% versus the same period in 2025, reflecting an unusual surge in exports and pullback in imports earlier this year (likely tied to tariff front-running).

**Short/long-term trends:** The trade deficit narrowed dramatically in 2020 as global trade froze, then widened to record levels in 2021–2022 as domestic demand for goods outpaced production capacity amid supply-chain snarls. 2025–2026 has been defined by tariff volatility, with importers and exporters repeatedly shifting the timing of shipments around tariff announcements and deadlines — producing exactly the kind of whipsaw seen here (a sharp one-month swing after months of a shrinking deficit). This is consistent with a broader post-COVID pattern in which trade data has become a noisier, more politically-driven series than in the pre-2020 era.

**AI tie-in:** Trade in AI-relevant categories — semiconductors, computing equipment, and industrial supplies feeding data-center construction — is one of the more closely watched threads in this data. The pullback in capital goods and computer exports in May bears watching, since AI-linked hardware (chips, servers, networking gear) has become one of the fastest-growing categories of U.S. trade in both directions; the U.S. imports huge volumes of chips and components (notably from Taiwan, with whom the U.S. runs a $19.4 billion monthly deficit) to build out domestic AI infrastructure, even as it exports design services and some finished AI hardware.

## June FOMC Minutes (Meeting held June 16–17, 2026; released July 8, 2026)

This was Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting leading the Committee, which voted unanimously (12–0) to hold the federal funds rate at **3.5%–3.75%**. The minutes revealed a Committee genuinely split on the path forward: some participants sketched a scenario where inflation eases enough to allow rate cuts, while others outlined a path where persistent price pressures (tariffs, energy, the Hormuz conflict) push the Committee toward hikes. Notably, participants discussed the impact of AI directly, observing that **"ongoing strong demand for AI infrastructure would likely sustain upward pressure on prices for technology products and electricity."** The Committee also debated shortening and simplifying its post-meeting communications, with several participants welcoming a review of the Fed's communication practices under new leadership.

**Short/long-term trends:** This meeting sits at the tail end of a multi-year arc: near-zero rates through the pandemic, the fastest hiking cycle in four decades through 2022–2023 to combat inflation, and a slow, halting path toward normalization since. Rates have now been parked at 3.5%–3.75% for all of 2026, reflecting a Fed caught between still-elevated inflation (driven partly by tariffs and energy shocks) and a labor market that's cooling but not breaking. The leadership transition to Warsh adds a new variable — a chair perceived as more inclined toward rate cuts and terser communication, operating under continued political pressure for lower rates.

**AI tie-in:** This is one of the more direct connections in this batch — the FOMC explicitly named AI infrastructure demand as an inflationary force, both through **technology-product prices** (chips, servers, cooling equipment) and **electricity prices**, as data centers strain regional power grids. Warsh has separately voiced the view that AI could ultimately prove disinflationary through productivity gains — but the minutes show the Committee currently treating AI-driven capex and power demand as a near-term upside risk to inflation, not a relief valve. This puts the Fed in an unusual position: AI investment is simultaneously a tailwind for growth/productivity and a source of price pressure the Committee has to monitor.

## May Consumer Credit Report (Federal Reserve G.19)

Total consumer credit was **essentially unchanged** in May on a seasonally adjusted basis. Revolving credit (mostly credit cards) **contracted at a 4.7% annual rate**, while nonrevolving credit (auto and student loans, etc.) grew at a modest 1.6% annual pace. This is a soft reading — consumers pulled back on credit card borrowing even as broader household finances look reasonably solid elsewhere: credit card APRs for interest-accruing balances rose to 22.15% in Q2 2026, and overall credit card balances have been trending down from their Q4 2025 peak.

**Short/long-term trends:** Consumer credit contracted briefly in 2020 as stimulus checks and reduced spending options paid down balances, then expanded aggressively through 2022–2024 as households leaned on cards to offset inflation — pushing card debt to record highs and delinquencies to their worst levels since the Great Recession. The past year has shown a cautious reversal: delinquencies have fallen for seven straight quarters, and revolving credit growth has cooled markedly (now outright contracting), suggesting consumers are more cautious about taking on new debt amid high APRs (which remain near 20-year highs) even as balances aren't spiraling.

**AI tie-in:** The connection here is more indirect but still real: elevated borrowing costs (a legacy of the post-COVID hiking cycle) are squeezing discretionary household credit even as record capital is flowing into AI infrastructure — a split between corporate capex (booming) and household credit (retrenching) that reflects two very different parts of the economy moving in opposite directions. If AI-driven productivity gains eventually show up in wages or lower prices, that could ease pressure on households; for now, though, consumers are deleveraging on revolving credit while corporations pour hundreds of billions into AI buildouts, financed largely through cash flow and debt markets rather than consumer spending.

## Initial Jobless Claims — Week Ending July 4, 2026 (released Thursday, July 9, 2026)

Initial jobless claims fell by 2,000 to **215,000**, below the consensus forecast of roughly 217,000–220,000 and a decrease from the prior week's upwardly revised 217,000. The 4-week moving average dropped to 218,750. Continuing claims, however, ticked up 8,000 to **1.814 million** for the week ending June 27 — the highest level since late March, though still historically low. Claims from federal employees continued to be watched closely given ongoing federal workforce reductions.

**Short/long-term trends:** Jobless claims spiked to unprecedented levels (in the millions per week) at the start of the pandemic, then fell to multi-decade lows through 2021–2023 amid a historically tight labor market. Over the past year, the picture has shifted to what's often described as a "low-hiring, low-firing" labor market: initial claims remain low (companies aren't doing mass layoffs), but continuing claims have drifted higher, meaning it's taking laid-off workers longer to find new jobs. That's the pattern again here — a good headline number masking a labor market where reemployment has slowed.

**AI tie-in:** AI is increasingly cited as a contributing factor to the "low-hiring" side of this dynamic, particularly in white-collar and entry-level knowledge work, where companies have cited AI-driven productivity and automation as a rationale for hiring freezes or headcount reductions (echoed in the ISM services report's mentions of hiring freezes). At the same time, the AI infrastructure boom is a major source of job creation in construction, electrical, skilled trades, and data-center operations — meaning the labor-market effects of AI are bifurcated: contractionary in some services/office roles, expansionary in physical infrastructure and skilled trades tied to the buildout.

## June Existing Home Sales Report (NAR, released July 9, 2026)

Existing-home sales **fell 2.4% month-over-month** to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of **4.09 million** in June, giving back some of May's 3.2% jump, though sales remained **2.8% higher year-over-year**. The median existing-home price hit a fresh record of **$440,600**, up 1.8% from a year earlier — the 36th consecutive month of annual price gains. Inventory slipped slightly to 1.56 million units (4.6 months' supply). Regionally, the Northeast was the only region posting a monthly gain (+2.1%), while the Midwest, South, and West all declined from May. First-time buyers made up 33% of June sales, up from 30% a year ago, and distressed sales remained near historic lows at just 2%.

**Short/long-term trends:** Existing-home sales cratered in 2020, then briefly boomed on ultra-low mortgage rates before the Fed's 2022–2023 hiking cycle pushed 30-year rates from under 3% to over 7%, collapsing sales volume to some of the lowest levels relative to population in modern history (the "lock-in effect," where owners with sub-4% mortgages won't sell). Sales have hovered stubbornly around a 4 million annual pace for roughly two years now — well below the pre-pandemic norm of ~5 million and far below the pandemic-boom peak of ~6 million — even as prices keep hitting records, a combination that reflects a supply-starved, affordability-constrained market rather than genuine demand destruction.

**AI tie-in:** Housing and AI intersect in a few ways worth noting: markets near major AI/data-center buildouts (parts of the Sun Belt, Pacific Northwest, and Northern Virginia) have seen localized home-price surges tied to tech-salary demand and construction-worker inflows — echoed in earlier 2026 reporting on AI-fueled price spikes in tech hub metros. More broadly, the same 10-year Treasury yield dynamics that set mortgage rates are influenced by the Fed's inflation outlook, which — per the June FOMC minutes — is itself partly shaped by AI infrastructure's effect on electricity and technology prices. In other words, AI capex is one of several threads now feeding into the interest-rate environment that's kept mortgage rates near 6.4%–6.5% and existing-home sales stuck near multi-decade lows.
